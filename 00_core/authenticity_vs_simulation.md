# AUTHENTICITY VS SIMULATIONThis file addresses the relationship between real media damage and deliberately created damage effects.## The distinction**Authentic damage**: produced by actual media conditions, hardware failures, material decay, or processing errors.**Simulated damage**: produced by digital tools, shaders, compositing, or AI prompting to imitate believable damage.## Both are validDAMAGE includes both. The repo is not a purity test.However, the relationship between them matters for:- believability- creative control- educational value- technical implementation## Authentic damage characteristics- unpredictable in detail- constrained by physical system limits- historically specific- often mixed with other artifacts in "native stacks"- may have unwanted severity or placement- carries real material history## Simulated damage characteristics- controllable in severity and placement- can mix across media systems (hybrid stacks)- can exaggerate beyond physical plausibility- can be "too perfect" or "too clean"- may miss the subtle wrongness of real failure## Simulation quality levels### Level 1: Cosmetic mimicrySurface appearance only. Recognizable signifiers (scanlines, RGB split, "film grain" overlay) without system logic.Risk: looks fake, flattening, generic.### Level 2: Behavioral simulationImitates how the artifact behaves: motion, timing, interaction with image content, severity response.Better: more believable, more controllable.### Level 3: System simulationModels the underlying system: phosphor excitation, tape magnetics, codec prediction, chemical response.Best: most flexible, most educational, most believable when pushed.## Repo guidance on simulationPrefer behavioral or system simulation over cosmetic mimicry.When simulating:- start from real artifact behavior- preserve cause-effect relationships- respect medium constraints unless deliberately hybridizing- avoid "perfect" uniformity—real damage is irregular## When authenticity matters- forensic analysis- archival documentation- historical recreation- educational contexts- "found footage" authenticity claims## When simulation is preferable- creative control over severity and placement- combining effects across media systems- exaggeration for expressive purposes- avoiding unwanted damage to real media- iterative design workflows## Hybrid authenticitySome of the most interesting work combines authentic and simulated:- real film damage scanned, then digitally extended- authentic VHS capture with added stylized artifacts- photographed textures applied as controlled overlaysMark these clearly:- "authentic base + stylized enhancement"- "hybrid analog-digital damage stack"## SummaryAuthenticity is the reference.Simulation is the tool.DAMAGE uses both, but stays grounded in the logic of real media failure even when inventing.

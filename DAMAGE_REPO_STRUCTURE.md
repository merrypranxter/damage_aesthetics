# DAMAGE REPO STRUCTUREThis file explains the intended architecture of the DAMAGE repository.The structure is designed to prevent the project from collapsing into a vague pile of "glitchy" references. The repo is organized by **medium**, **artifact family**, **cause**, **stacking behavior**, and **translation logic**.## Top-level structure- `00_core/`  Foundational definitions, boundaries, philosophy, and anti-drift rules.- `01_taxonomy/`  The master classification system for damage categories, severities, and cross-media behaviors.- `02_media_formats/`  Medium-specific files documenting how damage appears in specific formats and systems.- `03_damage_families/`  Cross-medium artifact families such as smear, noise, tearing, ghosting, contamination.- `04_processes_and_causes/`  Files describing what produces the damage: heat, dirt, duplication, decoding failure, etc.- `05_stacks_and_profiles/`  Common and useful combinations of damage effects.- `06_color_and_texture/`  Color logic, contamination palettes, phosphor behavior, chemical stain logic, and tactile surface qualities.- `07_composition/`  How damage alters framing, focal hierarchy, legibility, and subject perception.- `08_prompting/`  Prompt language and assembly strategies for image, video, animation, and hybrid systems.- `09_shader_and_fx/`  Translation of artifact behaviors into shader logic, post-processing, and compositing workflows.- `10_machine_readable/`  JSON, CSV, and structured lookup files for app logic and tagging systems.- `11_examples/`  Example profiles, case studies, and comparison logic.- `12_reference_intake/`  Instructions and templates for analyzing new references.- `99_repo_ops/`  Operational files for naming, authoring standards, and expansion planning.## Structural philosophyThis repo does **not** organize primarily by "looks."It organizes by:1. what medium is involved2. what artifact behavior is visible3. what caused it4. how it interacts with other damage types5. how it translates into generation or analysis workflowsThis prevents shallow categorization such as:- "glitch"- "noise"- "retro"- "damaged"- "weird"Those labels are too vague to be useful.## Why medium-specific organization mattersMany artifacts look superficially similar but come from different systems.Examples:- Vaseline diffusion ≠ tape smear- halation ≠ compression bloom- chroma bleed ≠ chromatic aberration- grain ≠ digital sensor noise- film burn ≠ clipped digital highlights- datamosh residue ≠ motion blurIf these are not separated by medium and mechanism, the taxonomy becomes sloppy and visually useless.## Why damage-family organization also mattersSome artifact behaviors recur across media in different forms.Examples:- ghosting- smear- tearing- contamination- instability- color misalignmentThe `03_damage_families/` section exists so these can be compared as behavioral families rather than isolated medium quirks.## Why causes matterDamage without cause awareness becomes decoration.Cause-based structure allows the repo to distinguish between:- physical contamination- storage decay- heat warping- playback failure- transmission errors- chemical reaction- generational duplication loss- intentional simulationThis makes the repo much more useful for prompting and for believable visual design.## Why stacks matterMost real damage is layered.A single image may contain:- tape noise- lens haze- chroma drag- overexposure bloom- frame jitter- recompression block artifactsThese layers interact. The repo must therefore support not only isolated effects, but **damage stacks** and combination logic.## Naming conventionsFilenames should be:- lowercase- underscore-separated- explicit- not cute- not redundantGood:- `chroma_luma_failures.md`- `ghost_frames.md`- `vaseline_diffusion_smear.md`Bad:- `glitch_stuff.md`- `retro_damage.md`- `cool_effects.md`## File writing principlesEach descriptive file should ideally include:1. definition2. visible traits3. causes / mechanisms4. common co-occurring artifacts5. severity range6. effect on legibility7. common emotional / perceptual effect8. prompting notes9. shader / FX translation notes10. neighboring confusion risksNot every file must be identical, but this structure is the target.## Recommended workflow for expansion1. lock core definitions2. lock taxonomy families3. build medium-specific files4. build cross-medium families5. build causes/processes6. build stack profiles7. build prompting layer8. build shader/FX layer9. build machine-readable system10. expand examples and intake templates## Drift risks to avoidThe repo should actively resist drifting into:- generic glitchcore- generic lo-fi nostalgia- generic analog fetish aesthetics- random cyberpunk overlays- "creepy VHS" as a catchall- decorative dirt with no system logic- digital-only bias- retro-only bias## Structural priorityWhen in doubt, prioritize:- specificity over vibe- mechanism over decoration- stack logic over single-effect thinking- believable failure over generic distortion- clear taxonomy over trendy naming## Short versionThis repo is a **museum of broken media behaviors**, not a junk drawer of "glitchy" references.

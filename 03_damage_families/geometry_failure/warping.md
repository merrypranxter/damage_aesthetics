# WARPINGThis file documents warping as a cross-medium geometry failure—continuous deformation of image space away from rectilinear projection.## DefinitionWarping refers to visible continuous deformation of image geometry, where straight lines become curved and spatial relationships distort in a smooth, non-random manner.## Core visual identityWarping feels:- continuous (not fractured)- curved or bent- spatially deformed- often radial or barrel/pincushion- lens or display-based- sometimes organic or fluid## Medium-specific manifestations### Lens (barrel/pincushion distortion)- radial curvature from optical design- barrel (bulging outward) or pincushion (pinching inward)- correctable in post but often left visible### CRT (screen curvature)- physical screen shape- electron beam geometry- corner distortion### Analog tape (edge warp)- mechanical tension issues- edge curling or waving- transport-related### Digital (algorithmic warp)- mesh deformation- fluid or elastic distortion- deliberate or corruption-based### Scanning (document warp)- paper curl or misfeed- non-flat original- perspective distortion## Warp types### BarrelBulging outward, corners stretched.### PincushionPinching inward, corners compressed.### MustacheComplex curvature, barrel near center, pincushion at edges.### WavePeriodic undulation, often mechanical.### SkewAngular slant, often from misalignment.## Severity rangeS1–S2: subtle curvature, minor edge bowingS3: obvious distortion, clear spatial deformationS4–S5: severe warping, extreme geometry transformation## Stack partnersOften with:- lens limitations- mechanical issues- perspective problems- display characteristics- deliberate stylization## Confusion risks### Warping vs tearingWarping is continuous; tearing is discrete displacement.### Warping vs perspectivePerspective is viewpoint-based; warping is medium-induced deformation.## SummaryWarping is the image space itself becoming plastic—geometry bending, curving, and deforming according to optical, mechanical, or algorithmic forces.

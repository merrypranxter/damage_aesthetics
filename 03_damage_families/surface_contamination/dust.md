# DUSTThis file documents dust as a cross-medium surface contamination artifact—particulate matter interfering with image capture or display.## DefinitionDust refers to visible particles, specks, or flecks introduced by contamination of optical paths, media surfaces, or scanning equipment.## Core visual identityDust feels:- particulate- discrete (individual spots)- randomly distributed- often sharp-edged- materially present- archival or neglected## Medium-specific manifestations### Film (emulsion dust)- black or white specks- frame-locked or dancing- projection or scan path contamination### Lens (front element dust)- soft spots or blobs- flare-inducing- usually minimal effect unless severe### Sensor (sensor dust)- dark or light spots depending on aperture- sharp at small apertures, soft at wide- fixed position in frame### Scanning (platen dust)- sharp or soft depending on focus- often high contrast- random distribution### Projection (path dust)- moving or static- magnified by optical path- often very visible## Dust characteristics### Size variation- tiny pinpricks to large blobs- often mixed scales### Brightness- dark dust (shadow-casting)- light dust (highlight or emulsion-side)- translucent dust### Movement- static (locked to medium)- dancing (loose in gate/path)- frame-to-frame variation## Severity rangeS1: few scattered flecksS2–S3: moderate density, noticeable contaminationS4–S5: heavy dust, major obscuration, archaeological neglect## Stack partnersOften with:- scratches- hair/lint- film grain- age-related degradation- poor storage conditions## Confusion risks### Dust vs grainDust is contamination; grain is native texture.### Dust vs noiseDust is discrete and often sharp; noise is distributed and statistical.### Dust vs dead pixelsDust can move or change; dead pixels are fixed to sensor grid.## SummaryDust is the archaeology of neglect—particles settling, accumulating, and marking the image with the passage of time and the failure of clean storage.

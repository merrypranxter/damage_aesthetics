# HOW TO ANALYZE REFERENCESThis file documents the systematic process for analyzing damaged media references and extracting structured DAMAGE taxonomy data.## DefinitionReference analysis is the forensic examination of damaged images or video to identify, classify, and document artifacts according to DAMAGE taxonomy.## Analysis workflow### Step 1: First impressionRecord immediate, untutored response:- What medium does this feel like?- What is the dominant damage impression?- What emotion or atmosphere does it convey?### Step 2: Medium identificationDetermine primary medium:- What system produced this?- What is the native format?- What is the display or capture chain?Evidence to examine:- Color response (film vs digital vs analog)- Texture character (grain, noise, block structure)- Edge behavior (sharp, soft, ringing)- Temporal behavior (if video)### Step 3: Artifact inventoryList all visible artifacts by family:**Noise:** grain, static, speckle, chatter?**Smear:** bleed, drag, optical, motion?**Tearing:** jitter, roll, sync loss?**Compression:** blocks, bands, ringing?**Contamination:** dust, scratches, stains?**Light:** bloom, halation, flare, leak?**Color:** misalignment, cast, dropout?**Geometry:** warp, curve, skew?**Temporal:** trails, ghosts, echo?### Step 4: Severity assessmentRate each artifact S0–S5:- Which dominate composition?- Which affect legibility?- Which are atmospheric only?### Step 5: Cause inferenceWhat produced these artifacts?- Native medium limitations?- Wear and handling?- Environmental damage?- Transfer/chain processes?- Deliberate stylization?### Step 6: Stack reconstructionBuild the damage history:- What is the base medium?- What native artifacts are present?- What additional damage was added?- In what order?### Step 7: Cross-verificationCheck for consistency:- Do artifacts co-occur logically?- Is severity appropriate to cause?- Are there contradictory elements?### Step 8: DocumentationRecord in standard format (see intake_template.md):- Visual description- Taxonomy classification- Severity ratings- Stack reconstruction- Prompt reconstruction## Analysis tools### Technical- Magnification for fine detail- Color sampling for cast analysis- Waveform/scope for video (if available)- Frame stepping for temporal artifacts### Comparative- Side-by-side with known examples- Severity ladder comparisons- Medium-specific reference libraries### Consultative- Expert verification for obscure formats- Historical context for era-specific damage- Technical documentation for format specifics## Common analysis pitfalls### Confirmation biasSeeing expected artifacts rather than actual ones.### Medium assumptionAssuming medium from content era rather than technical evidence.### Severity inflationOverrating damage due to emotional response.### Artifact confusionMisidentifying similar-looking artifacts from different causes.### Chain oversimplificationMissing intermediate steps in damage history.## Quality indicators for analysisStrong analysis:- Specific medium identification with evidence- Multiple artifact families identified- Severity appropriately calibrated- Coherent causal chain- Reproducible prompt reconstructionWeak analysis:- Vague medium ("old video")- Generic damage description ("glitchy")- Inconsistent severity- Contradictory causes- Unreproducible results## SummaryReference analysis is detective work—reading the technical history of damage from visual evidence, then translating that reading into structured, actionable taxonomy.

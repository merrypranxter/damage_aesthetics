# FOCAL POINT OBSCURATIONThis file documents how damage specifically affects focal points—faces, text, important forms—and the aesthetic consequences of such obscuration.## DefinitionFocal point obscuration refers to damage that specifically covers, degrades, or competes with the primary subject or important information in an image.## Types of focal obscuration### Direct coverageDamage physically covers the focal point.- Scratch across face- Blob or stain over text- Block corruption on important form### Competitive brightnessDamage brightness competes with subject.- Bloom overwhelming face- Light leak washing out subject- Overexposure from damage### Detail destructionDamage removes information needed to read subject.- Severe noise on facial features- Compression destroying text legibility- Smear removing defining edges### DistractionDamage draws attention away from subject.- Motion in artifact competing with subject motion- High-contrast damage in background- Rhythmic pattern competing with subject## Intentional vs unintentional obscuration### Unintentional (documentary)- Archival damage- Accidental contamination- Transmission errorsReads as: loss, tragedy, time's violence, authenticity### Intentional (stylized)- Deliberate placement- Severity control- Aesthetic purposeReads as: mystery, abstraction, emotional distancing, artistic choice## Obscuration severity and meaningS1–S2: Slight softening, mystery preservedS3: Clear interference, tension introducedS4: Severe degradation, subject threatenedS5: Near-total loss, subject becomes absence## Common focal obscuration scenarios### Portraiture- Scratches across face- Bloom softening features- Grain obscuring skin detail- Light leak washing out expression### Text/titles- Compression destroying legibility- Dirt covering characters- Smear distorting forms- Noise breaking letter shapes### Product/object- Reflection/glare obscuring surface- Damage removing identifying features- Color shift changing recognition- Physical damage breaking form## Obscuration and emotion### LossSubject present but damaged—memory fading.### MysterySubject partially hidden—curiosity, unease.### ViolenceSubject attacked by damage—aggression, tragedy.### AbstractionSubject becoming pattern—formal transformation.## Controlling obscuration### PlacementWhere does damage fall relative to focal point?### SeverityHow much information is lost?### TypeWhat kind of damage affects the subject?### ContextIs obscuration appropriate to message?## Prompting focal obscurationBe specific about subject and damage interaction:> "heavy scratches obscuring left side of face, light leak washing out eye detail, moderate grain softening skin texture, subject partially lost to damage"## SummaryFocal point obscuration is the most consequential damage decision—where the image hurts most, and what that pain means.

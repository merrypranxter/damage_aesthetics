# PHYSICAL HANDLING DAMAGEThis file documents damage caused by mechanical interaction with media—touch, transport, abrasion, and physical stress.## DefinitionPhysical handling damage refers to visible artifacts resulting from mechanical contact, transport, storage handling, or physical manipulation of media objects.## Categories### Abrasion- Surface wear from contact- Scratches, scuffs, polishing marks- Directional patterns from transport### Bending / flexing- Creases in paper/film- Crack patterns in emulsion- Delamination### Pressure damage- Crushing or compression marks- Embossing from contact with objects- Roller marks from transport### Touch contamination- Fingerprints on film/prints- Skin oils on optical surfaces- Saliva, food, environmental residue### Splicing / editing damage- Tape splices (analog video)- Film cement marks- Edit point degradation### Magnetic damage (tape)- Print-through- Bulk erasure- Partial demagnetization## Visual signatures- Pattern alignment with handling direction- Concentration at handling points (edges, leaders)- Gradation from high-contact to low-contact areas- Human-scale patterns (finger size, grip marks)## Common media### Film- Scratches from gate transport- Fingerprints on emulsion- Curl from humidity handling### Tape- Edge damage from misalignment- Creasing from poor winding- Sticky shed from binder breakdown### Prints / paper- Creases, folds, tears- Surface abrasion- Foxing from handling + humidity### Optical media- Disc scratches from handling- Delamination from flexing## Severity progressionS1: subtle wear patternsS2–S3: visible scratches, marks, contaminationS4–S5: severe abrasion, structural damage, information loss## Stack partnersOften with:- dust accumulation- environmental damage- age-related degradation- improper storage## Prompting notesUseful:- handling-worn film edges- fingerprint-contaminated optical surface- mechanically abraded print surface- tape transport scratches## SummaryPhysical handling damage is the archaeology of touch—every grip, transport, and contact leaving marks of human and machine interaction on the medium.

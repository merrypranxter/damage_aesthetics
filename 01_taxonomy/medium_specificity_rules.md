# MEDIUM SPECIFICITY RULESThis file defines how the DAMAGE repo preserves medium identity.Medium specificity is one of the core principles of the repo.Without it, the taxonomy collapses into generic damage language with no real analytical or generative power.## Why medium specificity mattersDifferent media fail in different ways because they are built from different materials, signal systems, storage logics, and playback mechanisms.Examples:- VHS stores and plays signal magnetically- film records chemically- JPEG simplifies pixel information through quantization- CRTs display through scan and phosphor excitation- lenses degrade through contamination, flare, and focus issues- sensors fail through readout noise, gain, clipping, and dead pixelsThese systems do not produce the same artifacts.## Core ruleNever describe a damage effect only by how it looks if the medium relationship can be specified.Weak:- smeary- noisy- distorted- glowy- degradedStronger:- chroma bleed from analog composite encoding- halation around bright edges in film stock- macroblocking from low-bitrate digital compression- grease-smeared diffusion across highlights- phosphor bloom on bright CRT elements## Medium-first classificationWhenever possible, identify:1. the medium2. the artifact family3. the cause or mechanism4. severity5. common companionsExample: Instead of:- "retro static noise"Prefer:- "analog video snow with soft luma instability and mild chroma contamination"## Similar-looking artifacts that should not be flattened together### 1. Grain vs noise vs snow- **film grain** = particulate, stochastic, stock-dependent texture- **sensor noise** = electronic noise, often shadow-heavy or chroma-speckled- **analog video snow** = unstable signal noise field- **compression noise** = quantization residue, often edge-biased or block-adjacentThese are not interchangeable.### 2. Halation vs bloom vs flare vs haze- **halation** = bright-edge glow due to light scattering in film/emulsion/backing structure- **bloom** = light expansion / clipping glow, often display or sensor related- **flare** = optical reflections/scattering in lens system- **haze veil** = diffuse loss of contrast from contamination or atmosphereAll are soft light phenomena. None are the same.### 3. Chroma bleed vs chromatic aberration vs color misregistration- **chroma bleed** = color information leaking or dragging, often in analog signal systems- **chromatic aberration** = lens-based color separation due to wavelength focus mismatch- **color misregistration** = misaligned print/display/channel geometryAll may look like color fringing. Their causes differ.### 4. Smear vs blur vs drag- **smear** = directional soft redistribution, often signal or material based- **blur** = broad loss of detail, may arise from focus or motion- **drag** = implied directional pull or lag- **bleed** = leakage of color/light/tonal information beyond intended boundsThese overlap but should not be treated as identical.### 5. Tearing vs warping vs jitter- **tearing** = discontinuity in image alignment or scan position- **warping** = continuous shape deformation- **jitter** = unstable position over time- **skew** = slanted or offset image structureThese are distinct structural behaviors.## Native vs imported artifactsA given medium has:- **native artifacts**: effects commonly produced by that medium itself- **imported artifacts**: effects introduced from another stage in the chainExample:A VHS image uploaded to social media may include:- native VHS softness- native chroma instability- imported digital recompression artifacts- imported platform bandingThis distinction matters.## Chain logic ruleIf an image contains artifacts from multiple systems, define the chain.Example:- film image scanned poorly then recompressed- VHS playback filmed off a CRT- digital video screen-recorded and re-uploaded multiple times- phone camera shooting through dirty glass and overcompressing the resultThe chain explains why multiple artifact families coexist.## Good specificity questionsWhen classifying an effect, ask:1. What medium is this native to?2. Could it occur in multiple media?3. If yes, how does its behavior differ in each?4. Is the effect primary or imported?5. What neighboring artifact is most likely to be confused with it?## Preferred language patternA strong artifact description often follows this structure:**[medium/system] + [artifact behavior] + [cause/mechanism] + [optional severity or companion effects]**Examples:- VHS chroma drag with mild horizontal sync instability- dirty-lens diffusion veil with flare contamination around highlights- low-bitrate H.264 macroblocking with temporal prediction smear- overscanned CRT bloom with faint convergence error- scratched negative with dust and light halation## Anti-flattening ruleIf two effects share a surface appearance but differ materially or structurally, keep them separate.The repo should always prefer:- exactness over convenience- structure over vibe shorthand- distinction over flattening## SummaryMedium specificity means the repo asks not only:**What does this damage look like?**but also:**What system does it belong to, and what kind of failure history does it imply?**

# SEVERITY SCALEThis file defines the shared severity system used across the DAMAGE repo.Severity describes **how strongly an artifact affects the image**, not merely whether the artifact is present.A subtle artifact and a catastrophic one may belong to the same family but produce totally different outcomes for readability, mood, and compositional use.## Purpose of the severity scaleThe severity scale helps with:- comparing artifact intensity- describing damage precisely- building prompt controls- constructing machine-readable tags- preserving legibility- designing damage stacks intentionally- separating texture from collapse## Severity is not one-dimensionalSeverity is not only about how visible an effect is.It also includes:- spread- frequency- contrast- persistence- effect on legibility- effect on color or geometry- dominance within the frameA low-visibility artifact can still be severe if it ruins important structural information.A very visible artifact may still be mild if it behaves decoratively and leaves the subject readable.## Shared scale### S0 — NoneNo meaningful visible artifact.Characteristics:- image appears clean or effectively clean- any imperfections are negligible- no artifact family meaningfully affects the imageUse cases:- baseline comparison- before/after demonstrations- control examples### S1 — TraceBarely perceptible presence.Mostly textural, atmospheric, or only visible on close inspection.Characteristics:- subtle grain, haze, or edge instability- slight optical contamination- faint compression softness- negligible impact on legibilityTypical effect:- suggests age, medium, or tactility without dominating the imageExamples:- barely-there grain- light lens grease bloom- faint recompression softness- subtle scan texture### S2 — MildClearly present but restrained.The artifact is noticeable and meaningful, but does not dominate the image.Characteristics:- visible but controlled- contributes to mood and medium identity- low to moderate effect on contrast, edges, or color- most subjects remain fully readableTypical effect:- adds realism, atmosphere, or tactile historyExamples:- moderate film grain- visible VHS softness- minor JPEG blocking in flat gradients- light chroma bleed- faint scanlines### S3 — ModerateStrong enough to become an active visual component. Damage is no longer background texture; it participates in composition.Characteristics:- obvious artifact structure- impacts edge clarity, color stability, or frame coherence- noticeable influence on focal hierarchy- partial subject interference possibleTypical effect:- stylistically significant- clearly "damaged" but still generally interpretableExamples:- obvious macroblocks- heavy tape smear- repeated ghost frames- strong haze veil- CRT bloom and convergence issues- visible scratches across important forms### S4 — SevereThe artifact strongly disrupts image integrity. Legibility and compositional stability are under significant threat.Characteristics:- damage dominates portions of the image- major detail loss- color or geometry may partially collapse- motion may become confusing- focal points may be compromisedTypical effect:- unstable, dramatic, expressive, or forensic- medium failure becomes central to the imageExamples:- major tracking tears- large datamosh fields- strong light leaks obscuring subjects- catastrophic scan banding- repeated frame freezes- severe sensor bloom and clipping### S5 — CatastrophicThe image is partially collapsing or functionally transformed by damage.Characteristics:- core visual information is lost, fragmented, or rewritten- medium failure overtakes content- geometry, timing, color, or structure may be deeply broken- readability may be low or highly unstableTypical effect:- ruin- breakdown- signal death- ecstatic abstraction- evidence of major system failureExamples:- full-frame corruption- massive packet-loss fragmentation- image split into unstable blocks- chemical destruction of film image- total sync collapse- extreme burn-through or decode failure## Domain-specific interpretationSeverity should be interpreted in context.### NoiseA moderate grain field may be S2.An equally visible sensor speckle field might be S3 if it wrecks shadows.### CompressionTiny blocking in a flat sky may be S2.Large blocks consuming faces may be S4.### Surface contaminationA fingerprint in the corner may be S1 or S2.Grease haze over the entire frame may be S3 or S4.### Temporal artifactsA faint motion trail may be S1.Persistent ghosting that confuses movement may be S4.## Severity dimensions to considerWhen assigning severity, ask:1. **Coverage**   How much of the frame does it affect?2. **Contrast**   How strongly does it stand out?3. **Persistence**   Is it momentary or continuous?4. **Structural impact**   Does it alter edges, color, timing, or geometry?5. **Legibility loss**   Does it make the subject harder to read?6. **Dominance**   Does it become a major visual actor?## Severity in promptingPrompt language should reflect severity levels.### Trace / mild- faint- subtle- light- delicate- barely-there- restrained- low-level### Moderate- obvious- pronounced- visible- active- strong enough to shape perception### Severe / catastrophic- heavy- aggressive- collapsing- dominant- severe- unstable- catastrophic- frame-consuming## Severity in machine-readable systemsSuggested machine values:- `S0`- `S1`- `S2`- `S3`- `S4`- `S5`Optional numeric mapping:- `0`- `1`- `2`- `3`- `4`- `5`Optional percentage mapping for UI controls:- `0–5%` = S0- `5–20%` = S1- `20–40%` = S2- `40–60%` = S3- `60–80%` = S4- `80–100%` = S5These percentages are heuristic, not literal physical measurements.## SummarySeverity answers:**How badly is the image being altered by this artifact?**Not:- is the artifact present at all- is the image pretty- does the effect feel "retro"Severity is about impact.

# DAMAGE FAMILIES OVERVIEWThis file gives a high-level overview of the primary artifact families used throughout the DAMAGE repo.A **damage family** is a cross-medium grouping of artifact behaviors that share visible logic, even when they arise from different sources.For example:- film grain- VHS snow- sensor noiseThese are not the same artifact, but they belong to a broader family of distributed noise interference.Damage families make it easier to:- compare related effects across media- avoid vague language- organize prompt logic- translate visual behavior into shader / FX categories- build machine-readable tagging systems## Why damage families existSome artifacts are medium-specific.Others recur across multiple media in structurally similar ways.Example:- optical haze- tape smear- compression smearing- motion dragThese may all produce "softened directional information," but they differ in cause, texture, and failure logic.The family system helps preserve both:- similarity- difference## The nine primary damage families### 1. NoiseNoise refers to distributed signal variation or particulate visual interference.It may appear as:- fine texture- crawling static- color speckle- luma roughness- grain swarms- random flicker fieldsNoise can be:- analog- digital- optical- sensor-based- compression-induced- material / particulateNoise often affects:- surface texture- shadow stability- low-light clarity- mood- realismExamples:- film grain- VHS snow- CCD speckle- chroma noise- dusty scan texture### 2. Smear / drag / bleedThis family includes directional spreading, dragging, leakage, or soft redistribution of image information.Common signs:- color drifting sideways- edge drag- motion softness with directional structure- luma/chroma separation- texture pulled into bands or trailsExamples:- chroma bleed- tape smear- codec smearing- optical grease smear- directional motion dragThis family is often confused with blur, but blur is too broad. Smear/drag/bleed implies movement or leakage behavior.### 3. Tearing / sync instabilityThis family involves timing failures, field mismatch, frame slip, or structural instability in playback/capture/display.Common signs:- horizontal tears- vertical roll- image skew- jitter- slipping bands- unstable line timing- warped frame lockExamples:- VHS tracking tear- sync drift- CRT roll- unstable frame alignment- digital buffer skip with positional discontinuityThis family often introduces agitation, instability, and mechanical tension into the image.### 4. Compression breakageThis family includes artifacts caused by encoding, decoding, quantization, resampling, and bandwidth limits.Common signs:- block segmentation- ringing around edges- mosquito noise- banding- gradient collapse- interframe melt- detail simplification- texture chunkingExamples:- JPEG macroblocks- H.264 smearing- low-bitrate streaming breakup- datamosh prediction residue- overcompressed screenshot decayThis family is especially important in contemporary web and social-media pipelines.### 5. Surface contaminationThis family includes visible interference caused by matter on or within the visual path.Common signs:- dust- lint- scratches- fingerprints- stains- residue veils- mold traces- grime depositsThese may sit:- on the lens- on the film- on the scanner glass- on the sensor- on the print- in the transfer pathSurface contamination often adds strong tactility and material presence.### 6. Light / exposure failureThis family includes artifacts produced by excess light, insufficient light, material-light interaction, or exposure errors.Common signs:- clipping- blown highlights- halation halos- bloom veils- washed exposure- buried shadow detail- flare contamination- light leaksExamples:- overexposed video bloom- film halation- clipped sensor highlights- accidental light leak- underexposure murkThis family strongly affects atmosphere, emotional tone, and perceived realism.### 7. Color failureThis family covers color corruption, channel instability, palette damage, and misregistration of color information.Common signs:- channel offset- false color- unstable hue fields- weird casts- chroma dropout- color crawl- palette collapse- mismatched edge colorExamples:- analog chroma instability- RGB channel misalignment- bad white balance pushed into ugliness- faded print color shift- dropped chroma channels in low-quality videoColor failure often interacts heavily with compression, analog video, and display systems.### 8. Geometry failureThis family includes distortions in spatial layout or the shape integrity of the image.Common signs:- curvature- warping- stretching- skew- malformed blocks- aspect distortion- rolling readout shape errors- line bendingExamples:- CRT barrel distortion- warped scan geometry- rolling shutter lean- stretched digital frames- block-grid deformation in corrupted videoGeometry failure changes the skeleton of the image, not just its surface.### 9. Temporal / memory artifactsThis family includes persistence, repetition, residue, or ghost information across time.Common signs:- afterimages- frame ghosts- motion echoes- repeat loops- persistence trails- freeze-and-release behavior- delay shadows- temporal dragExamples:- interframe residue- phosphor trails- ghost fields from analog playback- stutter-repeat buffering- motion persistence in long-exposure/video systemsThis family is crucial for video, animation, playback systems, and shader-based feedback.## Cross-family overlapArtifacts may belong to more than one family.Example:- analog chroma smear may involve both **color failure** and **smear / drag / bleed**- datamosh residue may involve **compression breakage**, **geometry failure**, and **temporal artifacts**- dirty lens bloom may involve **surface contamination** and **light / exposure failure**The families are not cages. They are organizational tools.## Using families in practiceEach artifact in the repo should ideally map to:- one primary family- zero or more secondary families- one or more medium domains- one or more causes- a severity range- typical stack partnersThis lets DAMAGE function as both:- a human-readable archive- a machine-usable classification system## SummaryThe damage-family system exists to answer this question:**What kind of visual injury is this, structurally?**Not:- what trend does it resemble- what era does it vaguely suggest- what aesthetic bucket it could be thrown intoThe family model keeps DAMAGE specific, scalable, and translatable.

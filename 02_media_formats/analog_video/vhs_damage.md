# VHS DAMAGEThis file documents the major damage behaviors associated with VHS and related consumer analog videotape playback systems.VHS damage is one of the most culturally recognizable damaged-media systems, but it is also one of the most oversimplified. This repo treats VHS not as a vague retro vibe, but as a specific cluster of analog signal, tape, tracking, chroma, and playback failures.## DefinitionVHS damage refers to the visible degradation, instability, softness, and signal contamination associated with magnetic videotape recording and playback in VHS-format systems and neighboring consumer tape formats.It includes:- native tape limitations- wear from repeated playback- tracking problems- head-switching artifacts- chroma instability- luma softness- tape noise- duplication loss- storage-related decay## Core visual identityVHS does not usually look "sharp but dirty."It usually looks:- soft- unstable- slightly smeared- chromatically weak- horizontally biased- noise-prone- time-worn- electrically vulnerableIts damage tends to feel mechanical, magnetic, and horizontally structured.## Common visible traits### 1. Luma softnessFine detail is blurred or absent.Edges lack bite.Textures may compress into mushy tonal regions.### 2. Chroma bleedColor spreads beyond luminance boundaries.Saturated areas may drag or leak horizontally.Red and blue regions are especially prone to looking unstable.### 3. Horizontal instabilityThe frame may jitter, ripple, or tear in narrow horizontal bands.The image feels scan-locked but only barely.### 4. Tracking noiseMisaligned playback produces rolling noise, unstable lines, tearing, or broad horizontal banding.### 5. Head-switching noiseA noisy band near the bottom of frame may appear where head-switching occurs during playback.### 6. Tape snow / analog noiseSignal weakness or tape wear can produce crawling noise fields, speckles, or unstable luma variation.### 7. Chroma/luma imbalanceColor and brightness may feel partly decoupled, producing dirty edges and unstable tonal-color relationships.### 8. Duplication lossRepeated copying causes additional softness, generational noise buildup, color weakness, and overall signal fatigue.## Typical artifact families involvedPrimary families:- smear / drag / bleed- tearing / sync instability- noise- color failure- temporal artifactsSecondary families:- geometry failure- light/exposure failure, if captured or transferred poorly## Common causes### Native format limitationsVHS is inherently limited in resolution and chroma fidelity.### Tape wearRepeated playback physically and magnetically degrades the recorded signal.### Bad trackingImproper tape/head alignment introduces tearing, instability, and noise.### Weak or dirty hardwarePlayback decks, cables, and heads may contribute additional artifacts.### Duplication chainsEach analog copy introduces more loss and noise.### Storage damageHeat, dust, humidity, and poor storage can worsen tape condition.## Typical co-occurring artifactsVHS damage commonly stacks with:- CRT display bloom- screen-capture moiré- dirty lens haze- analog audio-linked hum or instability- transcode compression if uploaded online- subtitle edge chatter if re-encoded- social-media recompression blur## Severity range### S1–S2- mild softness- slight chroma drift- light tape noise### S3- obvious tracking instability- clear chroma bleed- noisy lower frame band- repeat-generation loss### S4–S5- strong horizontal tearing- severe color breakup- unstable playback lock- major signal dropout- broad areas of visual collapse## Legibility effectsAt low levels, VHS damage adds:- nostalgia- tactility- softness- medium identityAt high levels, VHS damage can:- erase facial detail- distort text- destabilize motion- create false edges- obscure contrast boundaries- overwhelm the lower frame with noise## Emotional / perceptual qualitiesDepending on severity and context, VHS damage may feel:- intimate- domestic- ghostly- degraded- melancholic- trashy- haunted- ecstatic- forensic- familiar- unstableIt is not inherently horror-coded, though culture often treats it that way.## Confusion risks### VHS softness vs optical blurVHS softness is signal/detail loss, not lens defocus.### Chroma bleed vs chromatic aberrationVHS bleed is signal leakage, not lens-color separation.### Tape noise vs film grainTape noise tends to feel electronically unstable, not materially particulate.### Tracking tear vs digital glitch displacementTracking failure has analog horizontal scan/tape logic, not blocky computational structure.## Prompting notesUseful prompt phrases:- VHS playback softness- analog tape chroma bleed- mild tracking instability- worn videotape texture- head-switching noise near the bottom edge- duplicated VHS generation loss- unstable luma/chroma separation- soft consumer tape degradationAvoid empty phrases like:- VHS aesthetic- retro tape vibes- old video lookThose are too vague unless specified.## Shader / FX translation notesVHS-like simulation often requires multiple layers:- horizontal line instability- chroma offset or bleed- mild luma blur- lower-band noise- analog noise field- occasional sync discontinuities- optional playback jitterPure RGB split is not enough.Pure scanlines are not enough.VHS is a stacked analog playback system.## SummaryVHS damage is a medium-specific package of:- softness- chroma weakness- horizontal instability- analog noise- playback fragility- duplication fatigueIt should feel like tape trying, and partly failing, to hold an image together.

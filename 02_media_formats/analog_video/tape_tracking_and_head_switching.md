# TAPE TRACKING AND HEAD SWITCHINGThis file documents specific mechanical playback artifacts associated with analog videotape systems, particularly tracking errors and head-switching noise.These are distinct from general signal weakness or chroma instability—they are mechanical playback behaviors tied to tape transport and scanner geometry.## Definition**Tracking** refers to the alignment between the recorded tracks on tape and the playback heads. Misalignment produces visible instability.**Head switching** refers to the visible artifact produced when video heads hand off to each other during playback, typically appearing as a noisy band at the bottom of the frame.## Core visual identityThese artifacts feel:- mechanical- rhythmic or periodic- tied to tape transport- scan-line structured- playback-device specificThey are not random noise—they are systematic mechanical behaviors.## Tracking errors### Visible traits- horizontal banding or tearing- rolling noise bars- image skew or slant- unstable frame lock- periodic noise bursts synced to tape speed### Causes- misaligned playback heads- damaged or stretched tape- dirty or worn heads- incompatible tape/ deck combination- speed instability### SeverityS1–S2: faint occasional instabilityS3: regular visible noise bandsS4–S5: severe tearing, rolling, or inability to lock image## Head-switching noise### Visible traits- noisy horizontal band at bottom of frame- often 10–20% of frame height- may show chroma noise, luma noise, or line displacement- stable position (unlike tracking errors which may roll)### Causes- inherent to helical scan video systems- more visible on some decks than others- worsened by misalignment or dirty heads### SeverityS1: barely visibleS2–S3: clear noise bandS4: dominant bottom-frame artifact## Relationship to other VHS artifactsTracking and head-switching often coexist with:- chroma bleed- luma softness- tape noise- but are mechanically distinct from them## Prompting notesUseful:- misaligned tape tracking with rolling noise bars- visible head-switching noise at bottom edge- mechanical playback instability- helical scan tracking errorAvoid:- tracking glitch- tape noise at bottom- VHS static## SummaryThese are the mechanical scars of tape trying to mate with machine.They are periodic, structural, and transport-tied—not random signal mush.

# TONER DRAG AND DROPOUTThis file documents toner-based artifacts in laser printing and xerographic reproduction—specifically the ways toner fails to adhere, transfers incorrectly, or degrades across copies.## DefinitionToner drag and dropout refer to visible artifacts in laser-printed or xeroxed images caused by:- uneven toner distribution- drum or fuser irregularities- low toner conditions- paper texture interference- repeated copying degradation## Core visual identityThese artifacts feel:- powder-based- electrostatic- mechanically printed- office-machine specific- degrading across generations- sometimes gritty, sometimes smeary## Common visible traits### Toner drag- streaks or smears in direction of paper transport- uneven density bands- trailing edges of solid areas- partial fill-in of large dark regions### Toner dropout- light or white streaks where toner failed to adhere- speckled or incomplete solid areas- random or periodic voids- "snowy" texture in dark regions### Edge effects- toner buildup at edges of solid shapes- slight misalignment of edge density- halo or fringe around printed elements## Causes- low toner levels- worn drums or rollers- fuser temperature issues- paper humidity or texture- electrostatic interference- generation loss in repeated copying## Severity rangeS1–S2: subtle density variation, minor streakingS3: obvious drag patterns, visible dropoutS4–S5: severe streaking, major information loss, near-unreadable print## Confusion risks### Toner drag vs ink smearToner is powder-fused; ink is liquid absorption. Different texture and edge behavior.### Dropout vs paper textureDropout is toner failure; paper texture is substrate pattern.## Stack partnersOften with:- misregistration- copy-of-copy decay- scanner banding- paper grain texture## Prompting notesUseful:- laser printer toner streaking- xerox toner dropout and drag- electrostatic powder smear- repeated copy toner degradation## SummaryToner artifacts are the signature of electrostatic printing struggling with powder, heat, and mechanical transport—the image literally falling apart or dragging itself across the page.

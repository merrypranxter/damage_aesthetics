# PLAYBACK INSTABILITYThis file covers artifacts caused by unstable, damaged, or improper playback of media—distinct from the media's native damage or capture artifacts.## DefinitionPlayback instability refers to visible and temporal disruptions caused by:- mechanical playback variations- damaged or dirty playback heads- speed instability- tracking errors (beyond native format behavior)- buffer underruns or overruns in digital systems## Core visual identityThese artifacts feel:- mechanical in origin- temporally irregular- playback-device specific- sometimes rhythmic or periodic- like the machine is struggling## Common visible traits### Analog playback- wow and flutter (speed variation)- periodic noise bursts- head contact variations- tape tension irregularities- capstan or pinch roller issues### Digital playback- stuttering- frame drops or repeats- buffering artifacts- sync drift- decoder strain## Causes- worn or dirty playback equipment- mechanical misalignment- tape or media damage affecting transport- digital buffer issues- network streaming problems- decoder overload## Severity rangeS1–S2: subtle variation, occasional hiccupS3: obvious instability, regular disruptionsS4–S5: severe playback failure, near-unwatchable, mechanical chaos## Confusion risks### Playback vs media damagePlayback is the device struggling; media damage is the storage itself compromised.### Digital stutter vs datamoshStutter is playback/buffer; datamosh is compression/prediction.## Stack partnersOften with:- native media artifacts- capture artifacts if re-recorded- display artifacts## Prompting notesUseful:- unstable VHS playback- mechanical wow and flutter- digital buffer stutter- playback head contamination## SummaryPlayback instability is the machine's struggle to read—the moment of performance where device and media negotiate, sometimes unsuccessfully.

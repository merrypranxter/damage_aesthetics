# SCRATCHES, DUST, HAIRThis file covers the classic contamination and abrasion artifacts associated with film handling, projection, scanning, storage, and transfer.These artifacts are some of the most recognizable signs of physical media history because they feel bluntly material. They are not abstract distortions. They are evidence that matter got into the image path or that the film surface itself was physically injured.## DefinitionScratches, dust, and hair refer to visible contamination and abrasion artifacts introduced by:- damaged film surfaces- dirty handling- dirty projection paths- dirty scanners- poor storage- contact with fibers, particles, and debris- repeated transport through mechanical systemsThese are not part of the image capture process.They are intrusions on or injuries to the image.## Core visual identityThis family tends to feel:- tactile- mechanical- material- intrusive- path-dependent- physically specific- archival- aged or mishandledThey often read immediately as "something touched the film."## Main categories### 1. Surface scratchesLinear abrasions on the film surface or emulsion, often appearing as:- thin bright lines- thin dark lines- repeated vertical or near-vertical scars- intermittent streaking- frame-spanning abrasionsDepending on the workflow, scratches may appear:- sharp- soft-edged- bright- dark- broken- continuous### 2. DustSmall particulate contamination appearing as:- flecks- specks- tiny dots- clustered debris- random isolated particlesDust may be:- static or frame-locked- dancing due to transfer or gate movement- bright or dark depending on reproduction conditions### 3. Hair / fibers / lintElongated soft or sharp contaminants entering the optical or scan path.These may appear as:- curved strands- wispy arcs- fuzzy threads- looping fibers- soft translucent intrusionsHair often reads more bodily and accidental than scratches.## Common visible traits### 1. Verticality or transport alignmentScratches often align with the direction of film transport or scan path.### 2. Frame-spanning intrusionsAbrasions may persist across multiple frames or long image sequences.### 3. Random particulate interruptionDust appears as discrete interruptions of image clarity.### 4. Mechanical repetitionSome damage repeats in rhythmic or track-like ways due to projector/scanner path geometry.### 5. Surface-separate feelingThese artifacts often feel like they sit "on" the image or cut through it, rather than being native to scene content.## Main causes / mechanisms- rough handling- dirty film gates- dirty scanner glass- bad storage- mechanical abrasion during transport- projection wear- splices and handling damage- contact with cloth, paper, fibers, lint- neglected archival condition## Typical artifact families involvedPrimary:- surface contaminationSecondary:- geometry disturbance, if severe- light/exposure weirdness in scan/projection contexts- temporal persistence if particles recur across frames## Common stack partnersThis family often combines with:- grain- gate weave- flicker- light leaks- halation- faded color- duplication loss- dirty optics- scan softness- print wear## Severity range### S1–S2- occasional flecks- sparse scratches- minor lint or hair contamination### S3- clearly visible repeated scratches- moderate dust density- hair or fibers intruding on important areas### S4–S5- heavy abrasion- dense particulate contamination- major scratches across focal subjects- highly neglected or damaged archival appearance## Legibility effectsAt low levels:- add age and physicality- signal archival or mishandled media- enrich medium realismAt high levels:- obscure faces and text- distract from focal points- create unwanted false linework- interrupt large regions of scene information- make the medium itself overpower the subject## Emotional / perceptual qualitiesThese artifacts may feel:- archival- vulnerable- haunted- intimate- neglected- historical- tactile- worn- bodily- physically mortalHair and lint especially can feel weirdly intimate or gross because they imply human presence.## Confusion risks### Dust vs grainDust is contamination; grain is image-native particulate texture.### Scratch vs scan line artifactScratches are material abrasions; scan lines may be electronic or mechanical acquisition artifacts.### Hair vs motion streakHair/fiber intrusion is contamination in the image path, not subject motion.### Dust vs dead pixelsDust is irregular and material; dead pixels are device-specific and grid-aligned.## Prompting notesUseful phrases:- archival film dust and scratches- repeated emulsion abrasions- frame-spanning vertical scratch marks- particulate contamination in the film gate- stray hair and lint in the projection path- neglected print surface wear- soft fiber contamination over the image planeAvoid:- old movie scratches- dusty film look- vintage dirtToo broad. Too lazy.## Shader / FX translation notesSimulation should consider:- irregular spacing and scale- non-uniform brightness- frame persistence or intermittent recurrence- some artifacts sitting sharply, others softly- plausible transport direction- avoiding repetitive copy-paste dirt patternsHair/fiber artifacts especially benefit from:- curved or semi-random strand forms- slight softness or translucency variation- occasional edge fraying## SummaryScratches, dust, and hair are the blunt signatures of matter getting where it should not be and surfaces getting hurt where they should not have been touched.

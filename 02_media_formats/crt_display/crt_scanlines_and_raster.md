# CRT SCANLINES AND RASTERThis file documents the visible structural behaviors associated with CRT display raster, scanline formation, and screen-based image construction.CRT display artifacts are often lazily reduced to "scanlines," but the actual visual identity is richer than that. A CRT image is not simply a digital image with stripes laid over it. It is a time-based, beam-drawn, phosphor-excited display event with its own geometry, brightness behavior, and capture complications.## DefinitionCRT scanlines and raster refer to the visible line-structured organization of the image as drawn on a cathode ray tube, including:- horizontal line structure- raster spacing- beam-defined image construction- line-based brightness variation- interaction with screen capture and refreshThis category is about the display's structural image logic, not just nostalgic overlay.## Core visual identityCRT raster tends to feel:- line-built- electrically alive- slightly soft yet structurally segmented- beam-drawn rather than pixel-plotted- luminous- screen-bound- temporally activeA CRT image is often perceived as both:- continuous from a distance- discretely line-built up close## Common visible traits### 1. Scanline structureThe image is visibly organized into horizontal bands or rows, especially on bright regions, captured screens, or close-up display viewing.### 2. Raster spacingThe image does not feel like uniform square pixels. It has line spacing and beam-trace logic.### 3. Brightness modulation by lineSome lines may appear brighter or darker depending on content, scan behavior, or capture timing.### 4. Soft-luminous edge structureEdges may feel soft but energized, not merely sharp.### 5. Screen-door / capture interactionWhen re-photographed or screen-captured, raster structure can interact with sensor grids, producing moiré, aliasing, or beat patterns.### 6. Horizontal emphasisCRT display logic tends to accentuate horizontal organization, even when the overall image remains coherent.## What raster is notRaster is not the same as:- digital pixelation- video compression blocks- generic stripes- fake overlay "scanline filters"- print halftone lines- line tearingRaster is the structural drawing logic of the display itself.## Typical artifact families involvedPrimary:- geometry failure, when capture or convergence issues arise- light / exposure behavior- temporal / persistence behavior- subtle structural bandingSecondary:- color failure- moiré / interference during recapture## Common causes / mechanisms- cathode ray beam scanning- phosphor excitation by line- interlaced or progressive display behavior depending on system- display curvature and screen material- camera/display mismatch during recapture- refresh interactions- overscan / underscan behavior## Common stack partnersCRT raster often coexists with:- phosphor bloom- phosphor trails- convergence errors- barrel distortion- edge curvature- screen-capture moiré- brightness flare from photographing the screen- analog signal softness- broadcast instability## Severity range### S1–S2- faint visible scanline structure- soft line-built brightness behavior- subtle raster presence### S3- clear line construction- obvious recapture interference- prominent scanlines on bright regions### S4–S5- extremely visible scan structure- strong moiré- obvious capture mismatch- raster dominance over image contentNote: CRT raster itself is not inherently "damage." It becomes part of DAMAGE when its structural presence or associated capture artifacts materially affect the image.## Legibility effectsAt low levels:- adds display identity- adds tactile screen realism- reinforces period/device specificityAt higher levels:- reduces fine-detail clarity- introduces line-biased texture- competes with typography- creates false textures in recaptured footage- destabilizes perceived resolution## Emotional / perceptual qualitiesCRT raster may feel:- electric- immediate- nostalgic- alive- luminous- technical- warm- unstable when recaptured- strangely intimateIt often feels more "screen-present" than flat digital display.## Confusion risks### Raster vs scanline overlayReal CRT raster is structural and luminous; fake overlays are often dead, flat, and too uniform.### Raster vs interlacing artifactsRaster is display structure. Interlacing artifacts involve temporal field behavior.### Raster vs moiréRaster is native. Moiré arises when raster interacts badly with another sampling grid.### Raster vs pixel gridCRT does not behave like a modern square-pixel panel, even when the image source is digital.## Prompting notesUseful phrases:- visible CRT raster structure- beam-drawn scanline texture- luminous cathode-ray line construction- recaptured CRT screen with visible raster spacing- soft CRT line geometry- raster-visible display imageAvoid:- scanline filter- retro monitor look- old TV linesToo generic. Too dead. Too lazy.## Shader / FX translation notesTo simulate CRT raster convincingly, consider:- line-based brightness structure- subtle vertical falloff or slot logic depending on display type- soft beam response rather than hard stripe overlay- slight brightness bleed between lines- optional curvature and edge softness- optional interaction with phosphor bloom- optional recapture moiré when simulating camera-on-screen workflowsDo not just slap black bars over an image and call it a day. That's embarrassing.## SummaryCRT raster is the visible skeleton of the screen drawing itself line by line.It is not just "lines."It is display anatomy.
